Commercial Cleaning Standards: What's Expected in 2026

March 8, 2026 · 8 min read

The commercial cleaning industry has fundamentally changed since 2020. What was once primarily a cosmetic service has become a critical component of indoor air quality, occupant health, and regulatory compliance. This guide covers what commercial and industrial facility managers need to know about cleaning standards in 2026.

The Standards That Actually Matter

LEED Certification Requirements: LEED-certified buildings must maintain indoor air quality standards that include using Green Seal or ECOLOGO certified cleaning products, maintaining entryway mat systems, and documenting cleaning schedules. Failure to maintain these standards during the certification period can result in loss of certification.

Healthcare Facilities (Non-Clinical): Even non-clinical areas in healthcare facilities — lobbies, hallways, restrooms — face elevated cleaning requirements. Focus on high-touch surfaces, proper disposal of waste, and documented cleaning frequencies. Many healthcare systems now require cleaning staff to have bloodborne pathogen training.

Food Service Areas: Restaurants, cafeterias, and food prep areas require daily sanitizing of food-contact surfaces to NSF International standards (typically sanitizer concentration of 50-100 ppm for quaternary ammonium compounds).

What Your Janitorial Contract Should Specify

Generic "clean on sight" contracts are the #1 cause of cleaning quality failures. Your contract should specify: exact square footage of each area, exact tasks per area per frequency (e.g., "trash removal and surface disinfection of all desks and common areas daily, floor vacuuming three times per week"), quality inspection frequency (weekly), products to be used (by name/certification), equipment standards (HEPA-filtered vacuums required for facilities over 10,000 sq ft), and supervisor contact and response time for complaints.
