OSHA Compliance Checklist for Industrial Facilities

March 20, 2026 · 11 min read · by Pitt Facility Management

OSHA fines have increased substantially since 2016, with willful violations now reaching up to $156,259 per violation. For industrial and manufacturing facilities, a single OSHA inspection can result in six-figure penalties — not to mention the cost of business disruption, workers' compensation claims, and reputational damage. This checklist covers the 10 most common OSHA violations in industrial facilities and how to avoid them.

Top 10 OSHA Violations in Manufacturing and Warehouse Facilities

1. Fall Protection (1926.501) — Most Frequently Cited

Falls from elevation remain the #1 cause of death in construction and a major hazard in industrial facilities. Requirements: fall protection required at 4 feet (general industry) or 6 feet (construction) for walking/working surfaces with unprotected edges. Passive fall protection (guardrails) is preferred; personal fall arrest systems are acceptable when guardrails aren't feasible.

Action: Inspect all aerial lifts, scaffolds, and platforms. Ensure guardrails are in place and in good condition. Verify that all employees working at height have current fall protection training (annual requirement).

2. Hazard Communication (1910.1200) — Chemical Safety

Any facility using chemicals — cleaning supplies, solvents, paints, lubricants — must maintain a complete Hazard Communication (HazCom) program. This includes: Safety Data Sheets (SDS) for all chemicals, proper labeling, employee training, and a written program.

Action: Collect all SDS sheets and verify they're accessible to employees in all work areas. Audit all chemical containers for proper labeling. Ensure new employee HazCom training is documented. Update your written HazCom program annually.

3. Powered Industrial Trucks (1910.178) — Forklift Safety

Forklift-related violations are extremely common. Key requirements: operators must be trained and certified (every 3 years, or sooner if there's an incident), pre-shift inspections must be documented, and only approved attachments may be used.

Action: Maintain a forklift inspection log for each unit. Ensure operator training records are current. Post speed limits and traffic patterns. Verify that forklift battery charging areas have appropriate safety equipment (eye wash stations, fire extinguishers).

4. Electrical Wiring Methods (1910.305) — Wiring Violations

Extension cords used as permanent wiring, improperly rated circuit breakers, missing knockout covers, and damaged raceways are among the most common electrical violations found during OSHA inspections.

Action: Conduct a monthly inspection of all electrical panels: verify that all spaces are filled with proper covers, that circuits are properly labeled, and that no unauthorized modifications have been made. Replace all damaged cords immediately.

5. Machine Guarding (1910.212/219) — Equipment Safety

Moving machine parts, nip points, rotating shafts, and flying debris all require machine guarding. Guards must be substantial, secured, and not create additional hazards. Common violations: missing guards on exposed pulleys, chain drives, and gear boxes.

Action: Inventory every machine with exposed moving parts. Verify guards are in place, properly secured, and have not been removed for maintenance without lockout/tagout procedures being followed.

6. Lockout/Tagout (1910.147) — LOTO Procedures

Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) prevents the unexpected energization or startup of machinery during maintenance. LOTO violations often carry the highest per-violation penalties because the consequences of failure can be fatal. Requirements: written LOTO procedures for each piece of equipment, employee training, periodic inspections.

Action: Create or update your LOTO procedures for all equipment. Conduct annual LOTO audits (each piece of equipment, each procedure). Ensure locks and tags are readily available. Train all affected employees and maintain training records.

7. Respiratory Protection (1910.134) — Air Quality

If your facility has any area where airborne contaminants exceed permissible exposure limits (PELs), respirator use is required. This includes welding fumes, silica dust, chemical vapors, and wood dust. Requirements: written program, medical evaluation, fit testing, and training.

Action: Identify all areas where respiratory hazards exist. Conduct air sampling to determine actual exposure levels. If respirators are required, implement a full respiratory protection program — including the often-skipped medical evaluation and fit testing requirements.

8. Personal Protective Equipment (1910.132) — PPE

PPE violations occur when employers fail to assess hazards, select appropriate PPE, train employees on PPE use, and maintain/replace PPE as needed. Eye protection, hearing protection, hand protection, and foot protection are the most commonly cited categories.

Action: Conduct a formal PPE hazard assessment (documented in writing). Ensure PPE is provided at no cost to employees. Verify that PPE is being worn consistently — supervision, not just signage, is required for compliance.

9. Walking-Working Surfaces (1910.22) — Floors and Aisles

Wet floors, uneven surfaces, obstructed aisles, and missing floor plates are frequent violation categories. Aisles and passages must be kept clear. Surfaces must be clean and dry. Changes in elevation must be marked or guarded.

Action: Conduct a daily walk-through of production and warehouse areas. Establish and enforce 30-inch minimum aisle widths in warehouses. Address spills immediately. Mark changes in elevation clearly.

10. Emergency Action Plans (1910.38) — Fire and Evacuation

Every facility must have a written Emergency Action Plan covering fire, medical emergencies, and natural disasters. The plan must include evacuation routes, assembly points, employee accounting procedures, and emergency contact information.

Action: Update your Emergency Action Plan in writing. Conduct and document fire drills (at least annually). Ensure emergency exits are clearly marked, unobstructed, and properly illuminated. Verify that emergency contact information is posted in visible locations.

What Triggers an OSHA Inspection

Most OSHA inspections in industrial facilities are triggered by: a serious injury or hospitalization (employer must report within 24 hours for in-patient hospitalization, fatality within 8 hours), a complaint from an employee, a referral from another agency, or a planned inspection targeting a specific industry. Construction and manufacturing are two of OSHA's highest-priority inspection categories.

Getting Started with Compliance

Pick the two or three items from this list that you know are weakest in your facility and prioritize those first. The goal isn't perfection on paper — it's actual hazard reduction. Document everything. OSHA inspectors consistently say that the best evidence of a genuine safety culture is documentation: training records, inspection logs, equipment maintenance records, and written programs that are actually followed.
